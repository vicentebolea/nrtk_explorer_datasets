Description:
Abstract:
"This raster image data set presents color aerial photographs in an ortho-rectified format. The imagery was acquired March 15th and March 16th of 2006 and covers Cole County and a portion of Callaway County in Missouri. The digital orthoimagery was created from scanned aerial photography, survey control, aerial triangulation, and a digital terrain model. Mapping accuracy meets American Society of Photogrammetry and Remote Sensing (ASPRS) Class 1 Map Accuracy Standards for Orthophoto Maps 1 inch = 100 foot scale for Cole County and 1 inch = 200 foot scale for the NGA project area. The spacial coordinate system is NAD83, Missouri State Plane, Central, FIPS 2402, US Feet."
Purpose:
"The raster data depicts geographic features on the earth surface and is intended for reference within a Geographic Information System environment." These data have been created as a result of the need for having geospatial data immediately available and easily accessible in order to provide geographic reference for Federal, State, and local emergency responders, as well as for homeland security efforts. Orthoimages also serve a variety of purposes, from interim maps to field references for earth science investigations and analysis. The digital orthoimage is useful as a layer of a geographic information system. These data can be used to provide reference information for Web browsers and for map applications at a scale of 1:100,000 or smaller. Larger scale orthoimagery such as digital orthophoto quadrangles will be more accurate, but often at the expense of timely updates.

Process_Step:
Process_Description:
"The production of ortho-rectification of aerial photography required three components: the unrectified raw images produced from direct film scanning the original color negatives, camera calibration report, aerial triangulation calculations, and existing Digital Terrain Model (DTM) that was updated to reflect changes and/or new project areas. Rectification is accomplished by registering each digital image orientation to the ground. Breaklines and mass points, created from photogrammetric methods, was used to establish an accurate DTM to represent the earth surface. Ortho image rectification software was used attain imagery corrected for relief displacement, and to assemble individual images into a larger mosaic. The rectified imagery was enhanced for optimum brightness, contrast, and sharpness. Final imagery was examined for quality and accuracy through visual verification against source vector and surveyed control points."
